Give Yourself Grace (2-3 Minute Course)
1. Introduction (20-30 seconds)
   * Topic Introduction: Let’s talk about money managementnot just as a set of rules, but as a way to live with intention while allowing yourself to grow and learn.
   * Objective: We’ll discuss how to manage your money without tying your self-worth to financial success, how to create a budget that includes joy, and why it's okay to make mistakes along the way.
2. The Importance of Grace in Money Management (40-50 seconds)
   * Key Points:
      * Your Worth is Not Defined by Your Bank Account: It’s easy to tie your self-worth to how much money you make or how financially “successful” you seem, but remember: you are more than your bank balance. Your value as a person is not linked to your financial success. It’s important to recognize this as you navigate budgeting and saving.
      * Embrace Mistakes and Setbacks: No one gets everything perfect right away. Money management is a skill you develop over time, and mistakes will happen. Rather than seeing them as failures, view them as learning opportunities. You're allowed to be a work in progress. Growth is a journeyit's okay to stumble and pick yourself back up.
      * Room for Joy (1): While budgeting and saving are crucial, it’s also important to budget for things that bring you joy. Whether it’s a coffee out with a friend, a small hobby, or a weekend trip, allowing space for pleasure in your financial plans helps you stay balanced and motivated.
3. Personalized Budgeting (40-50 seconds) (2)
   * Key Points (3):
      * There’s No “One Size Fits All”: Different people have different experiences, so there’s no universal budgeting method that works for everyone. What’s right for someone else might not fit your financial situation or priorities. Your budget should reflect your own goals, values, and lifestyle.
      * Experiment and Adjust: Start by tracking your expenses and setting goals. Write down what you expect to spend and save, then compare it to your actual outcomes. This helps you adjust your budget and shift priorities when needed to find what works best for you.
      * Building Flexibility: Make sure your budget has room for flexibility. Life is unpredictableemergencies or opportunities might pop up. Allowing wiggle room[a] in your budget gives you space to respond without feeling anxious or rigid.
4. Real-Life Example or Story (40-50 seconds)
   * Scenario: Imagine a student named Sarah. She’s been sticking to a strict budget but often feels stressed because she can't save as much as others. One month, she goes over her budget to attend a concert with friends. At first, she’s upset, feeling the weight of the overspend, but then she realizes the experience brought her a lot of joy. Reflecting on the situation, Sarah acknowledges that while the concert was worth it, she could have saved money by cutting back on other expenses. She adjusts her budget to include more small joys while setting more realistic savings goals that align with her lifestyle. Despite the positive experience, she also considers how to better balance enjoyment and long-term financial goals in the future.
   * Key Points:
      * Sarah learned that life is about balance. Saving and budgeting are important, but so is living in the present and enjoying small moments.
      * She also realized that there’s no one-size-fits-all approach. Her budgeting journey is unique to her, and that’s okay.
5. Personal Reflection (40-50 seconds)
   * Guided Reflection: Ask yourself:
      * How do I currently feel about my finances? Do I feel pressure to manage them perfectly, or am I more relaxed with the understanding that I’m learning as I go?
         1. Can I learn what triggers my anxiety around personal finance topics as well as consider what I can do to mitigate stressful financial decisions. 
      * Have I allowed room in my budget for things that bring me joy, or do I feel like I’m only focused on saving?
         1. How long can I sustain without giving myself a reward? Can I be consistent and not fall for guilty pleasures?
      * What mistakes have I made in managing my money, and how can I view them as lessons rather than failures?
   * Encourage Students to Write/Journal: Spend a moment writing down your thoughts or goals for managing your money in a way that feels balanced and compassionate.[b]
6. Conclusion and Takeaways (20-30 seconds)
   * Summarize Key Points:
      * Remember: Your worth is not tied to your financial status. Money management is a tool for growth, not a reflection of your value as a person.
      * Create a budget that works for youthere’s no one-size-fits-all approach. Your financial plan should reflect your unique needs and values.
      * Give yourself grace. Mistakes will happen, and that's okay. Learn from them and keep adjusting.
   * Call to Action: Encourage yourself to start today. Reflect on what’s important to you and how you can manage your finances while still allowing room for joy and flexibility. Keep learning, keep growing, and remember that financial growth is a process, not a race.
