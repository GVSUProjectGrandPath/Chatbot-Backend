Installment Loans vs. Lines of Credit
 Introduction
* An overview of why understanding different types of credit is essential.
* Key focus areas: differences between installment loans and lines of credit, key terms, and collateralized vs. personal loans.
 Closed-End vs. Open-End Loans
* Closed-End Loans (Installment Loans)
 * Borrow a fixed amount and repay it over time in set payments.
 * Examples: Auto loans, student loans, mortgages.
* Open-End Loans (Lines of Credit)
 * Borrow as needed, up to a limit, and repay flexibly.
 * Examples: Credit cards, and home equity lines of credit (HELOCs).
 Key Loan Terms & Definitions
* Important terms:
 * Principal – The original amount borrowed.
 * Interest Rate – The cost of borrowing, expressed as a percentage.
 * Term – The length of time to repay the loan.
 * Minimum Payment – The smallest amount due (more relevant for lines of credit).
* Fixed vs. Variable Interest Rates:
 * Installment loans usually have fixed rates (same payment every month).
 * Lines of credit often have variable rates (payments change over time).
 Collateralized Loans vs. Personal Loans
* Collateralized Loans – Secured by an asset.
 * If you fail to pay, the lender can take the asset (house, car).
 * The lender prefers not to go this route because they never make the full money back from a repossession or foreclosure, but it helps reduce their loss so they offer better rates for collateralized loans
 * Examples: Mortgages, auto loans, secured personal loans.
* Personal (Unsecured) Loans – No collateral required.
 * Higher interest rates due to more risk for lenders.
 * Examples: Credit cards, student loans, personal lines of credit.
 Conclusion & Key Takeaways
* Recap: Installment loans = fixed repayment, lines of credit = flexible borrowing.
* Understand key terms like principal, interest rate, and term.
* Know the difference between secured vs. unsecured loans.
* Final takeaway: "Choosing the right loan depends on your needsfixed payments for big purchases, flexible credit for ongoing expenses!”
