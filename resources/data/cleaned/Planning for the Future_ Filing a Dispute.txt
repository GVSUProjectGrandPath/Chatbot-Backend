Filing a Credit Dispute
 Introduction
* Errors and fraud on credit reports can happen, and disputing them is an important step to protecting your financial health.
* Overview of key topics: how to file a dispute, freezing your credit, handling personal disputes, and how disputes affect your score.
 How to File a Credit Dispute
* Step 1: Get a copy of your credit report (AnnualCreditReport.com for free reports).
* Step 2: Identify incorrect information (wrong accounts, late payments, or identity theft).
* Step 3: File a dispute with the credit bureau(s) (Experian, Equifax, TransUnion):
 * Online, by phone, or via mail (include documentation).
 * Bureaus must investigate within 30 days.
 How to Put a Freeze on Your Credit Report
* A credit freeze prevents anyone from pulling your credit report without you removing the freeze or providing a PIN to view its new accounts from being opened in your name.
 * This reduces the likelihood of identity theft because even if someone uses your information to apply for a loan or credit card in your name, the loan officer can’t pull the credit report to process it
* Contact all three bureaus separately to freeze/unfreeze your credit.
* Free and doesn’t affect existing accounts or your credit score.
 Weighing Your Options When It’s Personal
* Many disputes involve someone you know (family, ex-partner, friend, roommate).
 * This is because they have the most opportunity to access your information vs a stranger
 * While this can be malicious, many times it is out of desperation. Ex: a parent with poor credit that uses a child’s social security number to get utility services for the family home
* Consider:
 * Will filing a dispute escalate personal conflict? If so, are you okay with that?
 * Can you resolve it directly before disputing?
 * If fraud is involved, filing a police report may be necessary.
 * Remember: Only you can decide what is right for you!
 How a Dispute Affects Your Credit Score
* Simply filing a dispute doesn’t hurt your score.
* If the dispute results in removing negative information, your score may increase.
* While under investigation, the account is marked as "in dispute," which could temporarily affect lending decisions.
 * You may need to provide documentation showing it is fraud to the lender so they know not to consider that loan when they are calculating your monthly payments to assess if you can afford the new loan.
 Conclusion & Final Takeaways
* Always check your credit report for errors and take action quickly.
* Freezing your credit is a strong protective measure if fraud is suspected.
* Consider personal relationships before disputing shared accounts.
* Final takeaway: "Your credit is your financial reputationprotect it by knowing your rights and taking action!"
