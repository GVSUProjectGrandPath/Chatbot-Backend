What Factors Do Loan Officers Consider?
Here’s a 2-3 minute video outline for your topic:

Video Title:
What Loan Officers Look for in Your Application

Introduction
* Quick greeting and hook:
"Thinking about taking out a loan? Let's talk about what loan officers actually check before they say yes or no!"

Part 1: Key Factors Loan Officers Review
 * Credit Score and Credit History:

 * Snapshot of your borrowing history.

 * Higher scores = lower risk = better loan offers.

 * Income and Employment:

 * Regular, stable income shows you can make payments.
 * Ensuring your budget can support the monthly loan payment. Loan officers will create an estimated budget using your income, the monthly expenses as they appear on your credit report, your monthly housing cost, and a standard monthly living expense for items that don’t appear on your credit (food, utilities, etc.)

 * Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI):

 * Total monthly debt payments compared to your income.

 * Lower DTI is better shows you aren’t overextended.

 * Revolving Debt Utilization
 * How much of your total credit limit on credit cards is available - maxed out credit cards is a sign of a tight monthly budget, looking for 30% or less of limit to be used

Part 2: Additional Considerations
 * Collateral (for secured loans):

 * Something valuable (like a car or savings) you offer as backup if you don’t pay.

 * Co-Borrowers and Co-Signers:
 * If applying jointly, how does their payment history, debt, and income contribute to or hurt your application
 * If unable to qualify alone, do you have a qualifying co-signer to assist in qualifying?

 * Savings and Assets:

 * Shows financial stability can you cover unexpected expenses?

 * Loan Purpose:

 * Why you need the money (buying a car, education, starting a business) can influence approval.
 * Using a loan for basic living expenses (paying rent, getting caught up on bills, etc.) is a sign that the budget can’t support another monthly payment

Part 3: Common Red Flags
 * Recent Job Changes:

 * Frequent moves can raise doubts about income stability.
 * Self-employment will require extra documentation such as multiple years of past tax returns to verify income is stable

 * High Credit Usage:

 * Using too much of your available credit can hurt your application.

 * Recent Late Payments:

 * Even one missed payment can lower your approval chances.
 * Collections for bills or loans in default

Conclusion
 * Encourage preparation:
"Want better chances of getting approved? Check your credit, organize your documents, and make smart money moves before applying!"

 First up, credit score and credit history. Think of it as your financial report card it shows how well you’ve handled borrowing in the past. Higher scores mean you’re seen as less risky, which can get you better loan offers.
Next is income and employment. They’ll check if you have steady, regular income and if your budget can realistically handle a loan payment. They even estimate your monthly living expenses, like rent, groceries, and utilities, to make sure it all adds up.
They’ll also look at your debt-to-income ratio, or DTI. That’s just your total monthly debt compared to your income. Lower DTI is better it shows you're not drowning in bills.
And don’t forget revolving debt utilization basically, how much of your available credit you're using. If your credit cards are maxed out, that’s a red flag. Ideally, you want to use less than 30% of your limit.
Part 2: Additional Considerations
 Depending on the loan, you might need collateral, like a car or savings account, to back it up.
If you're applying with someone else, like a co-borrower or co-signer, their financial info matters too their credit and income could either help or hurt your chances.
They'll also check your savings and assets. Having money set aside shows you're financially stable and can handle surprises.
Finally, the loan purpose matters. If you’re using the loan for something like buying a car or paying for school, that’s usually fine. But if you’re borrowing just to cover living expenses or pay overdue bills, that can signal trouble and make approval harder.

 A few things that can hurt you:
 * Recent job changes switching jobs a lot can make lenders nervous about your income stability.

 * High credit usage if you’re maxing out cards, it looks like you’re stretched too thin.

Thinking about taking out a loan? Let’s be real loan officers don’t just hand out money. Let’s talk about what they actually check before they decide if you're getting approved or not!
First thing they look at is your credit score and history. Basically, it shows how good (or not so good) you are at paying back money you borrow. A higher score equals less risk for them. And less risk for them equals better loan deals for you.
Then they check your income and job situation. They want to see that you’ve got steady money coming in and that you can actually afford the loan. They'll even estimate your budget based on your other loans, your rent payment, and living expenses.
Another big thing is your debt-to-income ratio that’s just how much of your monthly income already goes toward debt. The lower it is, the better.
And credit card usage matters too. If you’re maxing out your cards, that’s a red flag. Try to keep it under 30% of your limit if you can.
Also, keep in mind that if you’re applying for a secured loan, you might need to put up collateral like your car or some savings as backup. So the value of the car needs to make sense for the amount of the loan. They are not going to approve a $30,000 auto loan for a car that is worth $5,000!
Also, if you're applying with a co-borrower or co-signer, their credit and income get looked at too. So depending on their situation, it could help or hurt your application.
They’ll peek at your savings and assets to see if you’ve got money stashed away for emergencies. And yes, they do care what you’re using the loan for. Stuff like buying a car or paying for school is fine. But if you’re borrowing just to catch up on bills or rent, that could actually hurt your chances because it is a sign you might not be able to afford another payment.
Some stuff that can mess your approval chances up could include switching jobs a lot. It makes them wonder if your income’s stable. Having maxed out credit cards can make it seem like you’re barely making it month to month. And any recent late payments can be a sign that you are struggling with making ends meet.

Bottom line: if you wanna boost your chances, check your credit, pull your paperwork together, and make smart moves with your money before you apply. You’ve got this!
