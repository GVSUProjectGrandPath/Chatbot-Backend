Deciding to take out a loan
Introduction (30 sec)
Taking on a loan is a big decision. Before borrowing, it's essential to consider whether the debt is worth the investment, how much you can afford, and the role of co-signers, co-borrowers, and authorized users.
1. Is the Debt Worth the Investment? (45 sec)
Ask yourself:
* Will this loan help me achieve a long-term goal, such as education, a home, or a business?
* What are the total costs and how long will I have the loan, including interest and fees?
* How will it impact my financial future—credit score, savings, and overall debt load?
* Compare loan options, including federal vs. private loans, fixed vs. variable rates, and repayment terms.
2. How Much Loan Can You Afford? (45 sec)
Use loan calculators to estimate monthly payments (Bankrate). Adjust the loan term and interest rate to find a payment that fits your budget.
* Shorter terms = higher monthly payments but less interest paid overall.
* Longer terms = lower monthly payments but more interest over time.
* Incorporate the loan into your spending plan to ensure it aligns with your income and expenses.
3. Understanding Co-Signers, Co-Borrowers, and Authorized Users (45 sec)
* Co-Signer: Agrees to repay if you default. Their credit is at risk.
   * Good for first time borrowers that are working to build their credit and can’t qualify alone. Co-signer is someone that has a vested interest in your success like a parent, guardian, or someone that fills that role in your life
* Co-Borrower: Also called “joint”. Equally responsible for the loan. Debt impacts both parties.
   * Best for loans where both parties benefit from the loan, for example a couple or family members buying a home together or sharing a line of credit for household bills
   * Note: Even though if you have an agreement that you will split the cost, it impacts both of your credit the same. So the bank will not care who stopped paying, they want to be paid.
* Authorized User: Can use the credit but isn’t responsible for repayment. Typically used on credit cards vs other loan types
   * Best for situations where you want someone to have access to the funds from a credit card but don’t want to give them full account access. Typically a family relationship, such as a parent adding a child as an authorized user so they have a card for emergencies.
   * Still shows on your credit, so if the actual card holder keeps balances lows and pays on-time great way to build credit, but if they don’t it can still hurt your score even if you aren’t legally responsible for paying it
* Understand these roles before asking someone to join your loan agreement—missed payments can harm relationships and credit scores.
   * Really make sure you have trust and a clear agreement before taking on loans with others. Can damage relationships if it goes wrong.
Conclusion (30 sec)
Loans can be powerful financial tools, but they require careful planning. Consider long-term impacts, affordability, and who is involved in the borrowing process. Smart decisions today lead to financial stability in the future.

Script
As students, anytime the thought of taking a loan comes into mind for any reason, the first question to ask yourself is: Is this helping me reach a long-term goal? If it is for education, then you may be on the right track.
Firstly, analyze what you can afford. Just because you can borrow, doesn’t mean you should.
Use a loan calculator and adjust the interest rate, monthly payment and loan term. If it is for a shorter term, there are higher monthly payments with less interest overall, and if it's a longer term, it has lower monthly payments with more interest over time.
Know what you are venturing into. If it's federal, where there are low rates and more flexibility, or if the loan is private, where there are higher risks and fewer protections. Comparisons are key when making this decision. You always have to compare fixed vs. variable rates and check the repayment terms. The details matter!

Furthermore, know the difference between a co-signer, a co-borrower, and an authorized user. A co-signer helps you qualify but takes on risk if you don’t pay. A co-borrower is equally responsible. An authorized user can use the credit but isn’t on the hook legally, though it can still affect their credit.
As students as we are, we have to bear in mind that loans can open doors, but only if you plan smart, borrow responsibly, and understand who’s involved. Make sure it’s a decision you and anyone else attached to the loan can live with.
Key Takeaways:
* Is the Loan Worth It: Will it lead to long-term growth

* How Much Can You Afford: Use loan calculators

   *  Know the Roles: Co-Signer, Co-Borrower or  Authorized User
