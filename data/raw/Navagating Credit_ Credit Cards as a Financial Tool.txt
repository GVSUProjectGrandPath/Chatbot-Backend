Credit Cards as a Financial Tool
[0:00 - 0:15] Introduction
* Credit cards can be a powerful financial tool when used wisely.
* Overview of key topics: building credit, avoiding debt, selecting the right card, and maximizing rewards.
[0:15 - 0:45] Using Credit Cards to Build Long-Term Payment History
* [0:15 - 0:30] A credit card helps establish payment history, which is 35% of your FICO score. Because it is an open term loan, this allows you to long term payment history
* [0:30 - 0:45] Key habits for building credit:
   * Always pay on time to avoid late fees and damage to your score.
   * Keep credit utilization low (below 30% of your limit).
[0:45 - 1:15] Tips for Using Credit Cards Without Taking on Debt
* [0:45 - 1:00] Treat your credit card like a debit card—only charge what you can pay off in full.
* Pay off the full statement balance each month to avoid paying interest
      * A cash advance is when you transfer money from the credit card into your account, to a third party like a payment app (venmo, cash app), any transfer processed like an ACH (electronic check). Cash advance interest begins accruing immediately, so charged interest even if you pay the full balance each month
* [1:00 - 1:15] Set up automatic payments to avoid missing due dates.
   * If carrying a balance, prioritize paying more than the minimum to reduce interest costs.
   * If you don’t need to use credit much or are concerned about overspending, set up one recurring monthly bill to be paid with your credit card automatically (a streaming service, your phone bill, a utility, etc.) and an automatic payment for the credit card each month. That will prevent the card from going dormant and allows you to make on time monthly payments without taking on debt.
[1:15 - 1:45] What to Look for When Selecting a Credit Card
* [1:15 - 1:30] Consider:
   * Interest rate (APR): Lower is better if you might carry a balance.
         *    * Annual fees: Avoid unless the rewards outweigh the cost.
   * Credit limit: A higher limit can help with credit utilization.
* [1:30 - 1:45] Student or secured credit cards are great for beginners.
      * Some institutions offer preferred rates for students knowing that they will have lower scores to start but have higher earning potential in the future because they are pursuing education
      * Secured credit cards allow you to put your own money on hold equal to the credit limit of the card. This allows to the institution to offer lower rates like a collateralized loan while the borrower can begin building credit history
[1:45 - 2:15] Credit Card Rewards & Benefits
* [1:45 - 2:00] Types of rewards:
   * Cash back: Earn a percentage on everyday purchases.
   * Points & miles: Great for travel perks.
* [2:00 - 2:15] Use rewards responsibly—don’t overspend just to earn points.
      * Reward cards are best used if you are able to pay them off every month because you can get the rewards and not pay interest. If you plan to carry a balance, stick to a normal card so you pay less money in interest. Rewards cards tend to have higher rates and annual fees to offset the cost to the lender
[2:15 - 3:00] Conclusion & Key Takeaways
* Recap: Credit cards build credit, require responsible use, and can offer valuable rewards.
* Final takeaway: "Use credit cards as a tool—not a trap. Pay on time, spend wisely, and take advantage of the benefits!”


















Script


Let’s talk credit cards. Do you know that credit cards are not just for emergencies or rewards? But when used wisely, credit cards are a powerful financial tool.
One of the biggest benefits is ‘Building your credit history’. Your payment history makes up 35% of your FICO score, and credit cards help you build that long-term record. Just make sure to pay your bill on time and keep your balance low, ideally under 30% of your limit.
Here’s the golden rule: Treat your credit card like a debit card. Only charge what you can afford to pay off in full. Paying your full balance every month means no interest charges. And avoid cash advances, they start charging interest immediately, even if you pay the rest off.
Let me give you some quick and smart advice. If you’re worried about overspending, keep it simple. Use your card for one recurring bill like your phone bills and set up automatic payments. That way, you stay on track, avoid late fees, and build a consistent payment record.
Now, let us take note of this. You just don't have to just pick a credit card. Picking the right credit card matters. Consider these factors. 
* Go in for credit cards with lower interest rate (APR)
* Annual fees are only worth it if the rewards truly outweigh the cost.

* And with the credit limits, a higher limit helps with your credit utilization ratio.

As students, student cards or secured credit cards are great beginner options. Student cards often offer lower rates and benefits. Secured cards require a deposit, but they’re a safe way to build credit.
Last but not least, let’s not forget about rewards such as cash back, travel points, perks, etc. But don’t let rewards tempt you into overspending. They only pay off if you’re not carrying a balance. Otherwise, you’re losing money to interest.
My last words for you is use your credit card as a tool and not a trap.
Build your credit, avoid debt, and make the card work for you and not the other way around.
 Key Takeaways
   1. Build Credit Wisely: On-time payments and low balances improve your score over time.

   2. Avoid Interest: Always pay the full statement balance monthly.

   3. Skip Cash Advances: If Interest starts immediately, it’s a bad deal.

   4. Start Small: Use one recurring charge and auto-pay to stay on track.

   5. Choose the Right Card: Consider APR, fees, limits; beginners should explore student or secured cards.

   6. Be Reward-Smart: Only chase rewards if you pay off your card monthly. Make sure not to fall into the debt trap.
