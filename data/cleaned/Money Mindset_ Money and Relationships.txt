Money and Relationships (2-3 Minute Course)
1. Introduction
* Topic Introduction: Briefly introduce the relationship between money and interpersonal connections.
* Objective: Highlight how money can shape relationships, including friendships, family dynamics, and romantic partnerships.
2. The Impact of Money on Relationships
* Overview of Financial Impact on Relationships:
 * Statistics/Data:
 * Share key data on how financial stress can cause tension in relationships (3).
 * Discuss the role of financial inequality or differing financial habits in shaping relationships.
* Key Points:
 * Transparency in financial matters can lead to healthier communication and support.
 * Money can trigger powerful negative emotions in relationships involving control, respect, and self-worth. Everyone has different reactions according to their financial stability, these negative emotions can be turned into inspiration towards a new financial journey (1).
 * Financial insecurity remains a significant source of tension and conflict in romantic or family relationships (1).
 * ​​A recent study conducted by Credit Karma, a personal finance company, found that 88% of millennials have taken on debt after having spent time with a wealthier friend (2).
3. Case Study or Story
* Real-life Example or Hypothetical Case Study (4):
 * Scenario: An individual recounts their experience of lending a total of $120,000 to various friends and family members. Despite their generous support, these loans remained largely unpaid, leading to strained relationships and significant financial loss.
* Key Points:
 * Financial Strain: The substantial amounts lent resulted in personal financial difficulties, affecting the lender's ability to meet their own financial obligations.
 * Damaged Relationships: The lack of repayment led to feelings of betrayal and resentment, causing rifts with those who had borrowed money.
 * Emotional Toll: The situation caused considerable stress and disappointment, as the lender felt taken advantage of by people they trusted.
* Lessons Learned:
 * Set Clear Boundaries: Before lending money, establish explicit terms regarding repayment schedules and amounts to prevent misunderstandings.
 * Communicate Openly: Discuss financial matters transparently with friends and family to ensure mutual understanding and respect.
 * To bring up money respectfully, try a gentle, empathetic approach. For example, you could say, "Just checking in on the repayment. I understand if things are tight—let me know how it's going." This keeps the conversation open without pressure.
 * Be Prepared for Outcomes: Understand that lending money to loved ones can be risky; be prepared for the possibility that loans may not be repaid.
 * Protect Personal Finances: Ensure that lending does not jeopardize your own financial stability or lead to undue hardship. Individuals who seem more “picky” about their money seem to have an easier time with saying no, when they know lending money might affect them long term.
4. Personal Reflection
* Guided Reflection:
 * Ask students to reflect on their own financial mindsets and how they might influence their relationships.
 * Questions for Reflection:
 * How do I view financial success? Is it tied to stability, freedom, or something else?
 * Do I feel overwhelmed by financial struggles, or do I actively look for solutions?
 * How was money discussed in my family growing up? Was it an open conversation or a taboo subject?
 * What are my core beliefs about money (e.g., it’s a tool, a source of stress, a sign of success)?
 * How do I feel when I think about my own finances? Anxious, confident, uncertain?
 * How can I have meaningful conversations with my friends about finances?
 * How can my friends and I set mutual boundaries to support each other in our financial wellness?
* Encourage Students to Write/Journal: Give students a moment to jot down their answers, helping them connect their financial views to their social and romantic relationships.
5. Conclusion and Takeaways
* Summarize Key Points:
 * Money can deeply affect relationships, but awareness and communication can foster understanding and trust.
* Call to Action:
 * Encourage students to be mindful of their financial mindset and how it could influence their relationships.
 * Invite them to continue reflecting on how money fits into their personal and social lives, and how they can develop healthier financial habits in their relationships.
