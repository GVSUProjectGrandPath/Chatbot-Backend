Spending Plan Foundations (2-3 Minute Course)
1. Introduction
 * Topic Introduction: A spending plan is a powerful tool for managing your finances effectively. But how does it differ from a traditional budget? In this lesson, we’ll explore what a spending plan is, how it works, and how you can use it to take control of your money.
 * Objective: You'll learn the basic math behind creating a spending plan, the types of expenses to consider, and how the 50/30/20 rule can help simplify your financial planning.
2. Spending Plan vs. Budget (5)
 * Spending Plan: A spending plan is a flexible guide that helps you manage your income by prioritizing essentials, leaving room for personal needs and savings. Unlike a rigid budget, it adapts as your circumstances change—perfect for the unpredictable nature of college life.
 * Budget: Budgets are more structured and goal-driven. They work best when aligned with your lifestyle and financial priorities, but they can feel restrictive if your income varies month to month.
 * Key Difference: While both aim to help you manage money, a spending plan is often more realistic for students because it evolves with your situation. A strict budget requires tight control, which isn’t always feasible for those living on fluctuating or limited income.
 * How to Build a Student-Friendly Spending Plan:
1. Start with real data: Review 3–6 months of account history to find your average spending by category.
2. Identify non-negotiables: Rent, student loans, phone bills—things you must pay.
3. Calculate your net income: If you work hourly, average it out. Subtract your fixed costs to see what’s left.
4. Adjust and adapt: Reduce spending where possible and seek campus or community resources if needed (like food pantries or rent support).
5. Save what you can: Even if it’s not 20%, every dollar counts.
6. Struggling to meet basic needs? Explore DHS support or look into additional income streams like part-time work or short-term gigs.
3. Basic Math for a Spending Plan
 * Income: The first step is to calculate your total monthly income—this includes your salary, side gigs, financial aid, or any other sources of income.
 * Expenses: Next, list all your expenses—fixed costs (like rent, utilities, insurance) and variable costs (like groceries, entertainment, or personal items).
 * Math: Subtract your total expenses from your income. If your expenses are higher than your income, you’ll need to adjust your spending plan to avoid going into debt.
 * The 50/30/20 Breakdown (1): Apply the 50/30/20 rule to simplify the math:
 * 50% of income for needs
 * 30% for wants
 * 20% for savings and debt repayment
This breakdown ensures you're covering your essentials first, indulging in personal wants moderately, and saving for future needs.
 4. Types of Expenses to Consider (3)
 * Fixed Expenses: These are costs that remain constant each month, such as rent, mortgage, utilities, car payments, or insurance. These expenses are necessary and non-negotiable.
 * Variable Expenses: These costs can fluctuate monthly, including groceries, transportation, entertainment, and dining out. While these can be adjusted, they are still essential for managing your lifestyle.
 * Discretionary Spending: These are optional expenses like shopping for clothes, subscriptions (Netflix, Spotify), and vacations. These expenses should be part of your "wants" category in the spending plan.
 * Savings and Debt: Don't forget to include savings for future goals (emergency fund, retirement) and debt repayment. Allocating 20% of your income to this category ensures that you are securing your financial future.
 * Planning for Irregular Expenses: Also, remember to plan for irregular or seasonal expenses like gifts, vacations, or medical bills. These can be set aside in advance to avoid surprises.
 5. Conclusion and Takeaways
 * Summary of Key Points:
 * A spending plan is a flexible tool that helps you manage your income and expenses while keeping a balance between needs, wants, and savings.
 * The basic math involves tracking your income, calculating expenses, and applying the 50/30/20 rule for easy allocation.
 * Don't forget to account for both fixed and variable expenses, along with discretionary spending and savings.
 * Call to Action:
Create a spending plan today by starting with the 50/30/20 rule. Adjust it as needed based on your personal circumstances. This plan will help you feel more in control of your money and lay the foundation for better financial habits.
