Outline for 2-3 Minute Learning Module
Title: First-Gen College Students and Their Learning Journey: Navigating Money Personalities
1. Introduction (0:00 - 0:30)
* Briefly introduce the topic: First-gen college students face unique financial challenges shaped by generational knowledge gaps.
* Highlight why understanding one’s money personality (spender, saver, avoider, investor, etc.) is key to financial success.
* Understanding your habits and tendencies with money allows you to make informed financial decisions and makes you more aware of yourself when it comes to money.
2. Data & Gaps in Generational Knowledge (0:30 - 1:00)
* Present statistics on financial literacy gaps between first-gen and continuing-ed students.
 * First-generation students were significantly more likely to use both federal student loans (72% used source) and private student loans (28%) than continuing-generation students were (51% and 25%, respectively)
 * Continuing-generation students were more likely to use income from parent/family member(s) (78%) to fund their education than first-generation students (58%)
* Explain how financial habits are often learned at home, leaving first-gen students without key money management skills (e.g., budgeting, credit, loans).
3. Impact on College Performance (1:00 - 1:30)
* Discuss how financial stress leads to higher dropout rates and lower academic performance.
* A national survey revealed that 59% of college students have considered withdrawing from their studies due to financial pressures. This stress often leads students to reduce their course loads or leave college entirely, hindering their educational and career aspirations. https://www.ellucian.com/news/national-survey-reveals-59-college-students-considered-dropping-out-due-financial-stress
* Explain the connection between financial insecurity and decision-making (e.g., working long hours, avoiding financial aid opportunities).
4. Psychological Impacts (1:30 - 2:30)
* Imposter Syndrome: Feeling like they don’t belong, struggling with financial decisions others seem to "just know."
* Success Guilt: Pressure of “making it” for their family while feeling isolated in their struggles.
* How financial confidence can help combat these challenges.
5. Conclusion & Call to Action (2:30 - 3:00)
* Encourage self-awareness of money personalities to make informed financial choices.
* Share a resource (e.g., campus financial literacy workshops, budgeting apps, mentorship programs).
* End with a motivational takeaway: Understanding your financial journey is the key to thriving, not just surviving.

First Gen Students
 It’s hard enough being the first one in your family to go to college, but first generation college students face unique financial challenges as well.

Some people may think college tuition is the only financial difficulty that first-gen students face, but not understanding your money personality can lead to unexpected problems. Whether you enjoy spending or saving, this module will teach you to make informed financial decisions.

 First generation students are more likely to take out extravagant student loans and are less likely to receive financial support from their families.

This leads to students stressing about money, which can negatively impact their college experience by dragging down their grades or pushing them into dropping out altogether. Even worse, financial stress can cause psychological problems like imposter syndrome and success guilt.

 By learning your money personality, you can understand your spending habits and make informed financial decisions. With our FinLit budgeting tool, you can keep track of where your money is going and how it can be used.

 Remember: understanding your financial journey is the key to thriving, not just surviving.
